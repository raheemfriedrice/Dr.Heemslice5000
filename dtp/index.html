Before We Begin

You're not wrong for wondering.

Good boys are complicated. They're not bad people — they just caught feelings and now the dynamic is... a lot. You don't have to be mean about it. But you don't have to keep carrying it either.

Answer five honest questions and we'll tell you whether to pour it hot — a clear, kind conversation — or cold — some necessary distance.

1 of 5

01

How long has this good boy been lingering?

02

When you've pulled back, how does he handle it?

03

Outside of these feelings, does he genuinely add something good to your life?

04

Has he been told — directly or through very clear signals — that it's not happening?

05

Honestly — how are you feeling about managing this?

▲ Hot Water

He needs the heat.

Pour it warm — direct, but kind.

This one actually has some real value as a person in your world — he just got his signals crossed. A clear, caring conversation gives you both a reset without burning it all down. You don't have to lose him as a human. You just have to redirect the energy.

Say it plainly. Say it once. Say it with kindness. Hot water is still water — it moves things.

What that sounds like: "I genuinely value you as a person. I want us to be clear with each other so nothing gets weird. It's not like that for me, and I don't think it's going to be — but that doesn't mean you're not important."
▼ Cold Water

Time to cool it down.

Go cold — your peace comes first.

You've been patient. You've been kind. You've given this more of your energy than it deserved. Cold water doesn't mean cruel — it means clear.

Create the distance you need. Stop engaging with the hope. Let your absence say what another conversation won't. You don't owe anyone unlimited access to you. Some situations don't need more words — they just need space.

What that looks like: Shorter replies. Less availability. No mixed signals. Not because you're being mean — because you're being honest with yourself about what this is.
▬ Lukewarm

Somewhere in the middle.

One real conversation — then you'll know.

This one's sitting in a gray zone. The situation isn't urgent, but it's not nothing either. One direct, honest conversation will tell you everything you need to know about how this goes.

If he hears you and adjusts, the warm approach worked. If he doesn't, you'll have all the clarity you need to go cold. Either way — you come out knowing exactly where you stand. And that's worth more than staying comfortable in the gray.

One thing to remember: You're not responsible for managing his feelings. You're only responsible for being honest about yours. That's it. That's the whole job.
